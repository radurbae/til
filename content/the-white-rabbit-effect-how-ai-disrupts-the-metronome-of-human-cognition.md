Every generation invents technologies to save time and improve the quality of life, yet somehow each feels more rushed than the last. In 1971, Nobel Prize-winning economist and cognitive psychologist Herbert Simon noted a variation of this concept: “a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention.”

Today, we consider how AI may be the latest example of this paradox. It can summarize reports, answer questions, write code, analyze data, and generate ideas in seconds. If technology keeps making us more efficient, why do so many people feel they have less time to think?

The answer may lie not only in AI but in human intelligence. Throughout evolution, our brains have developed shortcuts that allow us to make decisions quickly. These shortcuts often save time in the moment but can create hidden costs later.

Cognitive biases compress thoughts to help us respond rapidly, but can distort judgment by over-indexing on survival over accuracy, leading to catastrophizing and threat amplification. Confirmation bias and selective attention optimize for emotional efficiency. For the same reason, stress sharpens attention but narrows our perspective, making instinctive reactions more likely.

Across biology, speed often comes at a cost.

Our technologies may be following a similar path. Artificial intelligence reduces the time needed to produce information, but it also shifts work elsewhere — to evaluating, verifying, refining, and deciding what deserves our attention. The result is a paradox: the more time we save, the more time can seem to disappear.

### **The White Rabbit Effect**

More than 160 years ago, Lewis Carroll captured this tension in an iconic character: the White Rabbit in *Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland.*

The Rabbit is remembered for one line: “I’m late.” Constantly checking his pocket watch, he rushes from one obligation to the next, never appearing to have enough time. Everything seems normal until Alice sees him, hears “I’m late,” and follows him down the rabbit hole. From that moment, reality changes. The Rabbit is the first sign that the ordinary world is about to disappear. Following him means entering an unfamiliar world with different rules-the Rabbit is a symbol of technological or societal disruption.

In 2022, many of us asked ChatGPT a harmless question out of curiosity, not knowing it might become our modern-day White Rabbit.

The White Rabbit appeared during another technological transformation. Britain was transitioning to railroads, factories, and standardized clocks. For the first time, people were expected to synchronize their lives to mechanical time rather than natural rhythms. Just like the White Rabbit, we were no longer guided by an internal sense of time or matching natural rhythms, but by external schedules that always seem to outrun us. The transformation ushered in by AI is not just changing the length of an hour but also changing the rhythm of human thought.

Today, social media has given the White Rabbit a second meaning. On platforms such as TikTok, the “White Rabbit” represents the last ordinary moment before life changes forever. AI may embody both interpretations. It is accelerating the pace of work while ushering us into a new relationship with time itself.

### **The Metronome of Cognition**

To understand why, it helps to distinguish between two phases of modern AI systems: training and inference. Training is the process by which a model learns patterns from massive datasets. Inference is the process by which the model is used in real time to answer questions, generate content, or complete tasks. Every time we interact with generative AI systems, we are interacting with inference.

Inference compresses the interval between question and answer. In that sense, it changes not only the speed of work but also the metronome of human cognition. It is through inference that AI begins to reshape our experience of time. Psychologists have long distinguished between [objective time (clock time) and subjective time](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/the-digital-self/202401/how-ai-may-redefine-our-understanding-of-time) (how we experience the passage of time). AI may not change the length of an hour, but it can fundamentally change our experience of it.

### **Why our Experience of Time Becomes Scarce**

The German sociologist Hartmut Rosa described modernity as a process of social acceleration, where technological advances increase speed but often leave people feeling more rushed rather than freer. AI does not simply accelerate tasks. It compresses the intervals between them. Inference systems take this one step further by reducing the delay between intention and output to almost nothing.

**The result is a shrinking of the present.**

Not in a philosophical sense, but in a practical one. The time between asking a question and receiving an answer approaches zero. The time between identifying a problem and generating a solution becomes shorter. The space in which reflection traditionally occurred becomes narrower.

Before these systems existed, most of our cognitive work had natural boundaries. Writing a report, analyzing a problem, or developing a strategy required periods of effort followed by pauses. Often, pauses were not wasted time but allowed for reflection, reconsideration, and mental recovery. Neuroscience shows that the intervals support performance— just as they do for athletes. They support memory, consolidation, and incubation. It also promotes activity in the default mode network (DMN), which plays a central role in creativity, self-reflection, and problem-solving. Now, when these get depleted, we often turn to AI, accelerating the cycle. The chase down the rabbit hole accelerates.

Recent research on human cognition provides another perspective on why this matters. Much of human thinking does not occur through conscious, step-by-step reasoning. Neuroscientists and psychologists have shown that many insights emerge during periods of reflection, incubation, or even apparent inactivity. AI systems increasingly compress these intervals. By eliminating waiting time, they cut the pauses that give us a chance to engage judgment, creativity, and insight.

**Now AI has compressed that pause.**

This creates a subtle but important inversion. The constraint is no longer production speed. It becomes cognitive bandwidth. The White Rabbit was not late because he lacked a watch. He was late because the watch defined the pace of everything around him.

Similarly, inference systems define a new pace of cognitive life. They establish an environment in which responses are immediate, expectations rise, and pauses become less frequent.

When a model can generate an answer in seconds, our workflows change. Instead of spending most of our time producing work, we spend more time reviewing, evaluating, refining, and responding to machine-generated output. The cycle becomes increasingly compressed: prompt, answer, revise, repeat. Our role has shifted from creator to continuous evaluator. That is, if we take the time to question AI output or take it as gospel.

**The Coal Question**

While AI may reduce the time required for a single task, it often increases the number of tasks that can be completed, reviewed, or expected within the same period. What looks like efficiency from the organization’s perspective can feel like overload. This is evocative of the paradox that William Stanley Jevons described in his book, which, oddly, was also published in 1865, *The Coal Question*: that making coal more efficient would lead to more, not less, coal consumption.

This helps explain a broader paradox in modern work and why so many people are reporting such high levels of stress. Email did not reduce communication pressure; it reduced the barrier to communication production and increased expectations of responsiveness. Smartphones did not reduce coordination effort; they extended it across the entire day and into the night. AI continues this pattern at a higher speed.

### **The New Bottleneck**

In the AI era, improving efficiency can mean more consumption rather than less, posing a challenge for companies trying to adopt AI. On the one hand, AI may reduce the cost of information generation while increasing the volume of information that demands human attention. This is creating a [dangerous bottleneck in science](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/378795463_Artificial_intelligence_and_illusions_of_understanding_in_scientific_research). AI can increase scientific output while creating the illusion of understanding, placing greater demands on constrained human evaluation skills.

Researchers have begun examining the cognitive consequences of this pattern. While AI reduces the effort required for specific tasks, it can increase the number of decisions that workers must make. Instead of creating information, people are increasingly asked to evaluate information. Instead of writing every sentence, they must determine whether AI-generated content is accurate, appropriate, and complete. In this respect, AI is moving work around and not just a time machine. It is a work distribution machine.

### **Cutting and Giving Ourselves Some Slack**

In *Alice*, the Rabbit is not simply moving quickly. He is responding continuously to a clock that never stops advancing. The Rabbit is already running, and Carroll never tells us where it winds up.

In contrast, the Alice in our illustration has an expression of curiosity rather than panic. She seems to be exerting her will — whether to follow the Rabbit into its hole or head for the open mountains. She, like us, still has the option to figure out the optimal speed for her human brain and titrate it so that speed produces relief rather than pressure. Some forms of life still deserve slower forms of time.
